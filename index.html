Power source

Array mass = P / (W/kg); area = P/(1361·η). A high W/kg makes the whole vehicle light, raising the vehicle specific power α = P/m_dry. Above α ≈ 100 W/kg the burn is short and near 1 AU, so the 1/r² fade barely bites and pure solar closes. The default uses ultra-thin GaAs (~1000 W/kg, Alta-Devices-class cells) — that single light-array lever lets a near-term ~4 kg/kW thruster close it (α ≈ 130). See "Vehicle specific power".
Fuel-cell efficiency assumed 60%.
RTG ≈ 5 W/kg; fission NEP (Kilopower→JIMO class) ≈ 25–50 W/kg. Constant power removes the 1/r² fade — the only pure-electric path that reaches the cruise floor.

How the numbers are computed

This is a first-order ("Fermi") feasibility model. Every formula below is implemented in the live calculator above and cross-checked against a validated Python engine (fermi_sim).

1 · Intercept geometry

A probe that has escaped the Sun coasts in a near-straight line at its heliocentric excess velocity v∞. To pass through where Alpha Centauri will be at arrival time T, it needs heliocentric velocity V_p(T) = A₀/T + V_ac — an aim term (point at AC's current position A₀, shrinking with time) plus a lead term (match AC's space velocity V_ac). AC's 3-D ecliptic state is built from its catalogued position (RA 14ʰ39ᵐ, Dec −60.8°, 4.344 ly), proper motion (3.69″/yr) and radial velocity (−22.4 km/s); this reproduces the known closest approach (≈ 28,000 yr at 3.1 ly).

|V_p| is smallest at the tangential intercept (~58k yr, where the aim term cancels AC's radial approach and only its ~23 km/s tangential motion remains). But AC sits 42° below the ecliptic with +11 km/s of out-of-plane velocity, so the tangential aim is tilted ~10° out of plane — and out-of-plane velocity cannot be borrowed from Earth. AC's track crosses the ecliptic at ~79k yr, where departure is purely in-plane. The exact departure-Δv optimum sits between them, at ≈72.8k yr (13.875 km/s); the round 75k yr used elsewhere is a near-equivalent benchmark, only ~10 m/s (0.07%) above that floor.

The arrival-time slider walks this curve. Because the design goal is the smallest spacecraft, the target is the minimum-propellant optimum at ≈73k yr, where the aim is nearly in-plane and Earth's 29.8 km/s is borrowed almost fully. Aiming sooner (toward 58k) or far later both cost extra Δv; any out-of-plane component must be bought with propellant, since it can't be borrowed from Earth. (The figures above are the impulsive Δv floor — a theoretical lower bound. The calculator's "required Δv" is instead the derived low-thrust Earth-escape spiral the ion engine must actually fly: Δv ≈ v_circ + v∞,E − 1.17 km/s, a closed-form fit of the integrated constant-tangential spiral, ≈ 25 km/s at the optimum. The ion engine has no impulsive kick, so it cannot capture the Oberth floor.)

Why does an out-of-plane aim raise Δv only modestly? A fair worry: a 10° tilt on a ~48 km/s departure vector is an ~8 km/s out-of-plane "plane change" — shouldn't that be huge? It isn't, because it adds in quadrature to an already-large in-plane excess, not on top of it. At 1 AU the probe needs heliocentric speed v_dep ≈ 48 km/s; subtracting Earth's in-plane 29.8 km/s leaves a ~18 km/s in-plane excess relative to Earth before any tilt. Adding the ~8 km/s out-of-plane component combines as √(18² + 8²) ≈ 19.4 km/s — only ~1.2 km/s more, which becomes ~0.8 km/s of extra LEO Δv. So the tilt does cost Δv (precisely why the optimum sits near in-plane), but because the excess vs Earth is already large, even 10° is a few-percent effect, not a doubling — and Δv changes least exactly where the curve is flattest, between the min-Δv point (~73k) and the ecliptic crossing (~79k). (This assumes best-case launch timing aligns the in-plane part with Earth's velocity; a fixed real launch date could cost more — see Limitations.)

Why isn't a longer flight slower? For a stationary target, more time means less speed — but AC is receding (4.5 ly at 58k yr → 8.4 ly at 100k yr). So the required cruise speed v∞ does not keep dropping with time: it bottoms out at the tangential intercept (~58k yr, 23.3 km/s — a hard floor; you cannot intercept AC any slower, ever) and then rises for later arrivals (23.8 at 75k, 25.1 at 100k). And the cheapest departure Δv (≈73k yr) is not the same as the cheapest speed (58k yr): the 58k aim is tilted ~10° out of the ecliptic, and that plane-change can't be borrowed from Earth, so it costs more Δv (14.65 km/s) than the slightly-faster but in-plane ~73k arrival (13.88 km/s). The chart at right plots both curves — Δv (left axis) bottoming at ~73k, and v∞ (right axis) bottoming at ~58k and rising.

2 · Departure energetics (LEO → v∞)

To leave on a heliocentric hyperbola of excess v∞, the probe needs heliocentric speed v_dep = √(v∞² + v_esc,☉²) at 1 AU (v_esc,☉ = 42.1 km/s). Earth supplies 29.8 km/s in-plane only; with best launch timing the excess relative to Earth is v∞,E = √(v_dep² + v_E² − 2·v_dep·v_E·cos β), β = out-of-plane tilt. From LEO the impulsive (Oberth) budget is Δv = √(v∞,E² + v_esc,LEO²) − v_circ,LEO ≈ 14 km/s floor. This 14 km/s is the departure budget, not the cruise speed — leveraging the LEO orbital speed, the perigee Oberth effect, and Earth's 29.8 km/s it produces a heliocentric cruise of v∞ ≈ 24 km/s (so 24 km/s × ~70,000 yr ≈ 5.5 ly, matching where AC is at arrival). Low-thrust ion spreads thrust over many revolutions, losing the perigee Oberth benefit and paying a spiral tax. The calculator now uses the derived naïve continuous spiral (constant tangential thrust, integrated by RK4 and reduced to the closed form Δv = v_circ + v∞,E − 1.17 km/s, matched to the integration within 0.5 m/s — see tools/fit_spiral.py and audit/calcs/audit_departure.py): ≈ 25 km/s at the optimum. This sits in the physical bracket between the ~14 km/s impulsive floor (a theoretical lower bound, unreachable by an ion engine) and the spiral itself. We also checked perigee-biased thrusting (plan 02 Phase B, audited): at this vehicle's ~milli-g thrust it is time-divergent — escape would take centuries, because perigee-thrusting raises apogee until the pre-escape orbits have unbounded period and the coast through them never ends. So perigee-biasing cannot lower the practical budget here (it needs a much higher-thrust stage), and the naïve always-on spiral is the realistic operating point.

Worked example — the Δv budget is not the cruise speed

A natural objection: "14 km/s for 70,000 yr only covers 3.3 ly, but Alpha Centauri is ~5.5 ly away by then — that doesn't add up." The resolution: 14 km/s is the departure Δv from LEO (the propellant budget), not the speed the probe flies at. That Δv is applied while already in orbit and deep in two gravity wells, so it converts into a much higher heliocentric cruise speed:

StepHowSpeed

So 14 km/s of onboard Δv buys a ~24 km/s heliocentric cruise — the gain comes from the 7.7 km/s you already have in LEO, the Oberth effect at perigee, and Earth's 29.8 km/s orbital velocity around the Sun. Distance check: 23.8 km/s × 70,000 yr = 5.5 ly, which is exactly Alpha Centauri's distance at arrival — it recedes from its ~3.1 ly closest approach (~28,000 yr from now) to ~5.5 ly at 70,000 yr (~6.0 ly at 75,000 yr). The probe is chasing a receding target, so its cruise speed must exceed AC's distance ÷ travel time.

Concept of operations (CONOPS)

Baseline architecture: direct solar-electric ion from LEO, Isp 3000 s, ~500 kg wet. The ion engine fires continuously from launch — first spiralling out of Earth, then thrusting heliocentrically to build the escape velocity — then shuts off for the long coast. All burning happens within a few AU of the Sun, where solar power is strongest.

5 · ArrivalPass within ≤ 2600 AU of AC (99% of the way); science/flyby—

Propulsion & power (baseline Isp 3000 s). Thruster input power ~5 kW (electrical); thrust ~0.20 N (204 mN); firing time ~1.1 yr continuous (0.9 yr at Isp 2000 s rising to 1.4 yr at 4000 s). Power comes from a ~18 m² / ~55 kg commercial silicon array (20% cells, ~3 kg/m², Starlink-class) delivering the 5 kW at 1 AU. Δv from LEO is the derived naïve low-thrust spiral ≈ 25 km/s (~14 km/s is the impulsive floor, a reference an ion engine can't reach; perigee-biasing is time-divergent at this thrust — plan 02 Phase B), for a wet mass of ~600 kg (~344 kg xenon, ~57% at Isp 3000 s).

This is slow, gentle thrust — not a fast burn. The acceleration is only ~0.04–0.08 milli-g (0.2 N on a ~500 kg craft), sustained for ~1 year to reach terminal velocity v∞ ≈ 24 km/s — not a few months. Over that year the probe makes many tight loops around Earth (the geocentric escape, weeks–months, <0.01 AU) and then roughly one loop around the Sun. It is precisely because the acceleration is so gentle and spread over ~1 year that the power (~5 kW) and propellant (~250 kg xenon) stay modest; reaching v∞ in weeks instead would demand megawatt-class power or a chemical stage with far more propellant — neither of which this design needs, since there is no schedule pressure against the 75,000-yr coast.

Is the thrust "enough to leave at the right time of year rather than aimlessly spiralling out"? Electric propulsion is inherently low-thrust — thrust-to-weight here is ~4×10⁻⁵, so departure is a ~1-yr spiral, not an impulsive kick. But for a direct interstellar departure there is no tight launch window to miss: the aim is a fixed inertial direction (where AC will be in ~75,000 yr), and AC's bearing drifts negligibly over a 1-yr spiral, so launch date is essentially free. The spiral is also not "aimless" — thrust is steered (continuous, tangential) to build v∞ in the chosen escape direction; the ~1 yr it adds is negligible against the ~75,000-yr coast. Launch timing only becomes a hard constraint for the optional architectures below (a Jupiter flyby needs alignment; a solar Oberth needs a perihelion drop).

3 · Mass & the rocket equation

Xenon propellant: m_p = m_dry·(e^(Δv/v_e) − 1), v_e = Isp·g₀. Electrical energy to expel it: E ≈ ½·m_p·v_e²/η — note this grows with Isp. Thruster force from input power: F = 2ηP/v_e; burn duration = m_p·v_e/F. For the derived ~25 km/s a vehicle is ~50–60% xenon at Isp 3000–4000 s (≈600 kg wet on a ~220 kg derived dry mass) — still comfortably feasible today.

Subsystem sizing. The solar array AREA = P / (S₀·η_cell / r²) with S₀ = 1361 W/m² (set by efficiency); its MASS = P / (W/kg), set directly by the array-specific-power slider. Default is commercial, buy-today silicon (~20% cells, ~91 W/kg at 1 AU; Starlink-class, not GaAs); the dropdown and sliders let you vary cell efficiency and W/kg (a concentrator reaches ~486 W/kg). The ion engine + PPU scales at ~6 kg/kW and the xenon tank at ~8% of propellant. The tool reports whether array + engine + tank fit inside the dry mass, leaving the remainder for bus + payload.

4 · The fuel-cell energy wall

Because EP energy scales with v_e, the power source is decisive. Chemical reactants store only ~MJ/kg, so a fuel cell needs m_react = E/(η_fc·e_chem) — tens of tonnes for this burn, and worse at high Isp. The mass-optimal fuel-cell EP point with separate reactants is ~1350 s; if the spent exhaust is itself used as propellant, v_e ≤ √(2η·e_chem) ≈ 2.4 km/s. A solar array delivering the same power masses only tens of kg. Conclusion: deep-space EP power must be solar (near the Sun) or nuclear-electric (a multi-kW reactor — an RTG is too low-power) — chemical fuel cells lose by ~3 orders of magnitude. Since the whole burn happens within a few AU of the Sun, solar is the natural match.

5 · Cruise time & gravity assists

Transit time is set by cruise v∞ (the years-long burn is negligible against an 80,000-yr coast): solving |A₀ + V_ac·T| = v∞·T gives arrival time. A Jupiter flyby can add ~10–15 km/s if Jupiter lies along the AC aim direction (rarely the case on a fixed schedule). A solar Oberth burn at a few solar radii is hugely leveraged — v∞ = √((v_peri+Δv)² − v_esc²) — so ~1–2 km/s yields the full 24 km/s, but needs a heat shield and a way to drop perihelion. Direct solar-electric remains the simplest route.

5b · Solar-Oberth, in depth

Diving to a small perihelion makes the spacecraft enormously fast; a burn applied there buys disproportionate energy (Oberth: Δv at high speed adds ≈ v·Δv of specific energy). At each perihelion the burn needed to reach the 23.4 km/s cruise floor is:

Buying the same 23.4 km/s at 1 AU (no dive) costs ~6 km/s, so 10 R☉ gives ~4.3× leverage and the headline burn falls to ~1.4 km/s. That leverage is real — but three caveats make solar-Oberth an assist-staged, heat-shielded, chemical-at-perihelion architecture rather than a cheap pure-ion shortcut:

  1. The perihelion burn must be high-thrust (chemical), not ion. The Oberth benefit exists only during the brief perihelion pass (hours). A ~0.2 N ion thruster would need ~58 days to deliver 1.4 km/s, so it cannot capture the effect — a small storable-chemical or solid kick stage is required at perihelion. So this path is not pure electric propulsion.
  2. Thermal limit sets the floor at ~10 R☉. ~1830 K demands a Parker Solar Probe-class carbon heat shield (Parker grazes ~9–10 R☉). Diving deeper (3–5 R☉) shrinks the burn further but pushes 2600–3350 K, beyond demonstrated shields — so the practical perihelion is materials-limited, not Δv-limited.
  3. Getting to perihelion is the expensive leg. Dropping perihelion from 1 AU to ~10 R☉ means shedding almost all of Earth’s 29.8 km/s of heliocentric angular momentum — via a Jupiter gravity assist or a multi-year Venus-assist tour (Parker used seven). That setup cruise and its targeting are the true cost; the 1.4 km/s headline burn is the easy part.

Net: solar-Oberth genuinely closes with a tiny burn, but it sidesteps the 1/r² power wall (heat shield + kick stage + assist tour) rather than solving it — the trade against the constant-power nuclear-electric route, which closes with pure ion but carries a reactor.

Limitations

  • Alpha Centauri is propagated as straight-line motion (galactic curvature negligible over 10⁵ yr / 1% miss).
  • Patched-conic geometry with best-case launch timing; the low-thrust departure is a naïve constant-tangential Earth-escape spiral (perigee-biasing was checked — plan 02 Phase B — and is time-divergent at this thrust, so always-on is the operating point), and no finite-burn steering losses beyond it.
  • Gravity-assist figures are geometric upper bounds, not phased trajectory solutions.
  • Costs are out of scope here (treated as a soft constraint); this tool optimises performance and mass.
  • All four trajectory panels draw the same path: one continuous low-thrust solar escape, numerically integrated (RK4, μ=4π², tangential thrust until v∞ is reached, then coast) and rotated so its escape asymptote points at the AC intercept — so the spiral opens smoothly into the cruise with no hard turns. The scrubber is log-scaled in distance (1 AU → AC), and every view renders only the flown path (never the future). The many-revolution part of the real mission is the geocentric Earth-escape spiral at <0.01 AU — far below these views' zoom — so at solar-system scale the heliocentric escape is correctly a sub-revolution arc.

  • UI render test. Playwright loads the page in a real browser and exercises the controls (audit/calcs/ui_playwright.py).
  • Third-party model re-implementations. Parallel Codex and Grok audits (audit/codex/, audit/grok/) rebuilt the physics from scratch — hand-built vs astropy, scipy vs grid/golden-section, separate integrators — and agree on every headline quantity to ≤0.1%.

Guide for an independent (human) auditor

To check the calculations yourself — and to surface anything the model omits — work through the steps below. Each headline number should be reproducible with a calculator or a few lines of code.

  1. Ephemeris. From AC's catalogued RA/Dec, distance, proper motion and radial velocity, build its 3-D heliocentric velocity and confirm space speed ≈ 32.3 km/s and closest approach ≈ 28,000 yr at ≈ 3.1 ly. (Its ~23.3 km/s tangential speed is the floor on cruise v∞.)
  2. Intercept. Test V_p(T) = A₀/T + V_ac by flying a straight line at V_p for time T and checking it lands on AC's position at T. Confirm |V_p| minimises at the tangential intercept (~58k yr) and the departure-Δv optimum is ~72.8k yr (13.875 km/s).
  3. Departure energetics. Reproduce the worked example: 14 km/s of LEO Δv → ~24 km/s heliocentric cruise (Oberth + Earth's 29.8 km/s). Check that 23.8 km/s × 75,000 yr equals AC's distance at arrival (~6.0 ly) — Δv budget ≠ cruise speed.
  4. Mass & power. Rocket equation for the xenon fraction; E = ½·m_p·v_e²/η for burn energy; solar area = P/(1361·η_cell) and mass = P/(W/kg). Confirm the dry-mass budget closes (array + engine + tank + bus + payload).
  5. Fuel-cell wall. Confirm chemical reactants (~MJ/kg) need tens of tonnes for the ~50,000 kWh burn — orders of magnitude over a solar array.
  6. Probe for what's missing. This is a first-order model; a rigorous audit should push on: (a) phased low-thrust at higher thrust-to-weight — the departure Δv is now the derived naïve spiral (~25 km/s); perigee-biasing was found time-divergent at this milli-g thrust (plan 02 Phase B), so a lower budget needs a higher-T/W stage, and a full finite-burn solution is still future work; (b) launch-window and calendar phasing (best-case timing is assumed); (c) gravity-assist phasing (the Jupiter/Oberth numbers are geometric bounds); (d) navigation, comms and component survival over ~75,000 yr; (e) thermal, radiation and propellant boil-off across the cruise; (f) primary-source validation of the cell, thruster and tank assumptions. File findings against audit/.

How the calculator works — what drives what

There is no hidden state and no "run" step: every time you move a control the whole chain below recomputes in one pass. The dependency graph, in order:

  1. Aim geometry. Arrival time → where AC is (and how fast) at that time → the required heliocentric velocity V_p → cruise v∞ and out-of-plane tilt.
  2. Required Δv. v∞, tilt, LEO altitude + architecture (direct / Jupiter / Oberth) → departure Δv from LEO, derived from the integrated low-thrust Earth-escape spiral (Δv = v_circ + v∞,E − 1.17 km/s); the impulsive Oberth floor is a reference lower bound only.
  3. Minimum propellant. Δv, Isp → mass ratio (Tsiolkovsky) → minimum propellant fraction; with dry mass → propellant & wet mass — the spacecraft being minimised.
  4. Power & subsystems. power, Isp, efficiency → thrust & burn time; power, cell efficiency, array W/kg → array area & mass; power × engine kg/kW → engine; tank % × propellant → tank.
  5. Mass closure. dry mass − (array + engine + tank) → bus + payload budget.
  6. Optimum. A scan over all arrivals finds the minimum-Δv (least-fuel) point (~73k yr) and how far the current aim is above it.

Which slider moves what:

  • Arrival time — the whole trajectory: tilt → Δv → fuel. The main optimisation knob.
  • Dry mass — scales the vehicle in absolute kg; does not change the arrival or the propellant fraction (only the absolute kg of propellant, array, etc.).
  • Isp — higher ⇒ less propellant for the same Δv, but more electrical energy per kg.
  • Power — thrust, burn time and array size (not Δv or arrival).
  • Departure Δv — derived from the integrated low-thrust Earth-escape spiral (no penalty knob; the impulsive floor is a reference only).
  • Cell efficiency / array W/kg / engine kg-per-kW / tank % — subsystem masses only.

When is a design impossible? Two hard limits flip it to NOT FEASIBLE:

  • Power gate (decisive) — the achievable cruise v∞ saturates below the floor. Pure solar-electric always fails this (1/r² fade); nuclear-electric clears it at the right power. See "Power-limited v∞".
  • Mass diverges — with the derived minimal dry mass, every kg of propellant drags a fixed mass of tank + structure; if that times the mass ratio exceeds 1, the propellant + structure spiral has no finite solution (the rocket-equation wall). Reproduce it by dropping Isp toward ~1000 s.

In the default ~73k-yr, 5 kW, Isp-3000 s configuration the margins are comfortable, so it reads feasible — push those sliders to the edges to see the limits bite.

Repository structure

Everything is open-source (MIT) at github.com/fermiexplorer/fermi. The Python engine is the source of truth; the web calculator is a parity-checked port of it. Key paths:

  • fermi_sim/ — Python engine (source of truth): astro (AC ephemeris), intercept (aim geometry), departure (LEO→v∞ Δv), spacecraft (mass/power), trajectory (cruise + assists).
  • run_analysis.py — the integrated analysis report (stdout).
  • index.html + web/physics.js — this interactive calculator and its shared physics (value-by-value parity-checked against Python).
  • audit/ — independent verification: calcs/ (55 Python checks + a Node JS↔Python parity check), a gmat/ cross-validation of the departure model against NASA's GMAT propagator (scripts, comparison & raw outputs), and parallel model re-implementations codex/, grok/, gemini/.
  • docs/ — REPORT.md (tender feasibility report), engineering plans/, and the adversarial audit prompts.
  • tests/ — pytest smoke/regression tests.
