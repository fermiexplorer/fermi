How the numbers are computed

This is a first-order ("Fermi") feasibility model. Every formula below is implemented in the live calculator above and cross-checked against a validated Python engine (fermi_sim).

1 · Intercept geometry

A probe that has escaped the Sun coasts in a near-straight line at its heliocentric excess velocity v∞. To pass through where Alpha Centauri will be at arrival time T, it needs heliocentric velocity V_p(T) = A₀/T + V_ac — an aim term (point at AC's current position A₀, shrinking with time) plus a lead term (match AC's space velocity V_ac). AC's 3-D ecliptic state is built from its catalogued position (RA 14ʰ39ᵐ, Dec −60.8°, 4.344 ly), proper motion (3.69″/yr) and radial velocity (−22.4 km/s); this reproduces the known closest approach (≈ 28,000 yr at 3.1 ly).

|V_p| is smallest at the tangential intercept (~58k yr, where the aim term cancels AC's radial approach and only its ~23 km/s tangential motion remains). But AC sits 42° below the ecliptic with +11 km/s of out-of-plane velocity, so the tangential aim is tilted ~10° out of plane — and out-of-plane velocity cannot be borrowed from Earth. AC's track crosses the ecliptic at ~79k yr, where departure is purely in-plane. The exact departure-Δv optimum sits between them, at ≈72.8k yr (13.875 km/s); the round 75k yr used elsewhere is a near-equivalent benchmark, only ~10 m/s (0.07%) above that floor.

2 · Departure energetics (LEO → v∞)

To leave on a heliocentric hyperbola of excess v∞, the probe needs heliocentric speed v_dep = √(v∞² + v_esc,☉²) at 1 AU (v_esc,☉ = 42.1 km/s). Earth supplies 29.8 km/s in-plane only; with best launch timing the excess relative to Earth is v∞,E = √(v_dep² + v_E² − 2·v_dep·v_E·cos β), β = out-of-plane tilt. From LEO the impulsive (Oberth) budget is Δv = √(v∞,E² + v_esc,LEO²) − v_circ,LEO ≈ 14 km/s floor. This 14 km/s is the departure budget, not the cruise speed — leveraging the LEO orbital speed, the perigee Oberth effect, and Earth's 29.8 km/s it produces a heliocentric cruise of v∞ ≈ 24 km/s (so 24 km/s × ~70,000 yr ≈ 5.5 ly, matching where AC is at arrival). Low-thrust ion spreads thrust over many revolutions, losing the perigee Oberth benefit and paying a spiral tax; a naïve continuous spiral (integrated numerically, RK4) costs ~25 km/s, while optimised perigee-biased thrusting lands near 20 km/s (a benchmarked industry figure). The realistic budget is therefore bracketed by the ~14 km/s impulsive floor and the ~25 km/s spiral bound; the low-thrust-penalty slider is the sensitivity control across that range. The ~20 km/s point is an engineering benchmark, not derived here — a full phased finite-burn optimisation is future work.

Worked example — the Δv budget is not the cruise speed

A natural objection: "14 km/s for 70,000 yr only covers 3.3 ly, but Alpha Centauri is ~5.5 ly away by then — that doesn't add up." The resolution: 14 km/s is the departure Δv from LEO (the propellant budget), not the speed the probe flies at. That Δv is applied while already in orbit and deep in two gravity wells, so it converts into a much higher heliocentric cruise speed:

LEO circular speed (already orbiting)√(μ⊕/r)7.7 km/s

hyperbolic excess leaving Earth√(v_peri² − v_esc,⊕²)18.6 km/s

So 14 km/s of onboard Δv buys a ~24 km/s heliocentric cruise — the gain comes from the 7.7 km/s you already have in LEO, the Oberth effect at perigee, and Earth's 29.8 km/s orbital velocity around the Sun. Distance check: 23.8 km/s × 70,000 yr = 5.5 ly, which is exactly Alpha Centauri's distance at arrival — it recedes from its ~3.1 ly closest approach (~28,000 yr from now) to ~5.5 ly at 70,000 yr (~6.0 ly at 75,000 yr). The probe is chasing a receding target, so its cruise speed must exceed AC's distance ÷ travel time.

3 · Mass & the rocket equation

Xenon propellant: m_p = m_dry·(e^(Δv/v_e) − 1), v_e = Isp·g₀. Electrical energy to expel it: E ≈ ½·m_p·v_e²/η — note this grows with Isp. Thruster force from input power: F = 2ηP/v_e; burn duration = m_p·v_e/F. For 20 km/s a ~500 kg vehicle is ~40–50% xenon at Isp 3000–4000 s — comfortably feasible today.

Subsystem sizing. The solar array is sized physically rather than by a fixed W/kg: area = P / (S₀·η_cell / r²) with S₀ = 1361 W/m², and mass = area · areal density. Defaults are commercial, buy-today silicon (~20% cells, ~3 kg/m² → ~90 W/kg at 1 AU; Starlink-class, not GaAs); the dropdown and sliders let you vary cell type, efficiency, and areal density. The ion engine + PPU scales at ~6 kg/kW and the xenon tank at ~8% of propellant. The tool reports whether array + engine + tank fit inside the dry mass, leaving the remainder for bus + payload.

4 · The fuel-cell energy wall

Because EP energy scales with v_e, the power source is decisive. Chemical reactants store only ~MJ/kg, so a fuel cell needs m_react = E/(η_fc·e_chem) — tens of tonnes for this burn, and worse at high Isp. The mass-optimal fuel-cell EP point with separate reactants is ~1350 s; if the spent exhaust is itself used as propellant, v_e ≤ √(2η·e_chem) ≈ 2.4 km/s. A solar array delivering the same power masses only tens of kg. Conclusion: deep-space EP power must be solar (near the Sun) or nuclear/RTG — chemical fuel cells lose by ~3 orders of magnitude. Since the whole burn happens within a few AU of the Sun, solar is the natural match.

5 · Cruise time & gravity assists

Transit time is set by cruise v∞ (the years-long burn is negligible against an 80,000-yr coast): solving |A₀ + V_ac·T| = v∞·T gives arrival time. A Jupiter flyby can add ~10–15 km/s if Jupiter lies along the AC aim direction (rarely the case on a fixed schedule). A solar Oberth burn at a few solar radii is hugely leveraged — v∞ = √((v_peri+Δv)² − v_esc²) — so ~1–2 km/s yields the full 24 km/s, but needs a heat shield and a way to drop perihelion. Direct solar-electric remains the simplest route.

Limitations

  • Alpha Centauri is propagated as straight-line motion (galactic curvature negligible over 10⁵ yr / 1% miss).
  • Patched-conic departure with best-case launch timing; no detailed finite-burn or steering losses beyond the additive low-thrust penalty.
  • Gravity-assist figures are geometric upper bounds, not phased trajectory solutions.
  • Costs are out of scope here (treated as a soft constraint); this tool optimises performance and mass.
  • The 3D view plots true ecliptic coordinates in light-years (drag to rotate, scroll to zoom); the play/scrubber uses log-scaled time so the ~1-yr departure is visible against the ~75,000-yr coast. The 2D "Earth escape" stage is schematic (not to scale); "Sun escape" and "To Alpha Centauri" are to-scale top-down projections onto the ecliptic.

Audits & independent verification

Every headline number is cross-checked several independent ways — a value is only trusted when it agrees across methods:

  • Engine ↔ web parity. The browser math (web/physics.js) is checked value-by-value against the Python engine fermi_sim (audit/calcs/audit_webjs.mjs, 10 checks).
  • Independent suite (41 checks). audit/calcs/run_audits.py re-derives results by a different method than the engine: AC ephemeris via astropy; the intercept optimum by brute-force search plus a forward-propagation loop-closure; departure Δv by energy conservation; the rocket equation by numerical mass-flow integration; the fuel-cell optimum by independent search; solar sizing from first principles.
  • UI render test. Playwright loads the page in a real browser and exercises the controls (audit/calcs/ui_playwright.py).
  • Third-party model re-implementations. Parallel Codex and Grok audits (audit/codex/, audit/grok/) rebuilt the physics from scratch — hand-built vs astropy, scipy vs grid/golden-section, separate integrators — and agree on every headline quantity to ≤0.1%.

Guide for an independent (human) auditor

To check the calculations yourself — and to surface anything the model omits — work through the steps below. Each headline number should be reproducible with a calculator or a few lines of code.

  1. Ephemeris. From AC's catalogued RA/Dec, distance, proper motion and radial velocity, build its 3-D heliocentric velocity and confirm space speed ≈ 32.3 km/s and closest approach ≈ 28,000 yr at ≈ 3.1 ly. (Its ~23.3 km/s tangential speed is the floor on cruise v∞.)
  2. Intercept. Test V_p(T) = A₀/T + V_ac by flying a straight line at V_p for time T and checking it lands on AC's position at T. Confirm |V_p| minimises at the tangential intercept (~58k yr) and the departure-Δv optimum is ~72.8k yr (13.875 km/s).
  3. Departure energetics. Reproduce the worked example: 14 km/s of LEO Δv → ~24 km/s heliocentric cruise (Oberth + Earth's 29.8 km/s). Check that 23.8 km/s × 75,000 yr equals AC's distance at arrival (~6.0 ly) — Δv budget ≠ cruise speed.
  4. Mass & power. Rocket equation for the xenon fraction; E = ½·m_p·v_e²/η for burn energy; solar area = P/(1361·η_cell) and mass = area × areal density. Confirm the dry-mass budget closes (array + engine + tank + bus + payload).
  5. Fuel-cell wall. Confirm chemical reactants (~MJ/kg) need tens of tonnes for the ~50,000 kWh burn — orders of magnitude over a solar array.
  6. Probe for what's missing. This is a first-order model; a rigorous audit should push on: (a) a full finite-burn / phased low-thrust trajectory (the ~20 km/s SEP figure is a benchmark, not solved here); (b) launch-window and calendar phasing (best-case timing is assumed); (c) gravity-assist phasing (the Jupiter/Oberth numbers are geometric bounds); (d) navigation, comms and component survival over ~75,000 yr; (e) thermal, radiation and propellant boil-off across the cruise; (f) primary-source validation of the cell, thruster and tank assumptions. File findings against audit/.

Starlink: SpaceX has not published Starlink's cell efficiency or array specific power, so the ~20% silicon and ~90 W/kg figures here are representative commercial values, not a SpaceX specification.
